Consider the Culinary Apprenticeship Solution

Daniel Boulud is one of the “culinary rock stars” who got their start in the industry as an apprentice.

Culinary apprenticeship has long been a gateway into the industry. Apprentices train under a supervising chef in a restaurant, in a large scale institution, or in the armed forces. They work in all sorts of kitchens, from fine-dining to chain restaurants and gastro-pubs, from hotels and resorts to private clubs and cruise lines, and in a wide variety of contracted foodservice operations.

And thanks to the oft-cited shortage of cooks, chefs, and other culinary workers with rock-solid skills, apprenticeship is getting renewed attention.

For example, renowned French Chef and NYC restauranteur Daniel Boulud has been a strong advocate for increasing culinary apprenticeships to help solve the skilled workforce shortage. In 2015, through his business, The Dinex Group, Boulud created a European-style apprenticeship program with a focus on bringing more high school students into the industry.

With their promise of helping to decrease workforce turnover and increase employee loyalty, many in the industry are looking at the culinary apprenticeship solution, creating training programs that provide educational instruction, mentoring, and on-the-job experience through station rotation.

These include registered apprenticeship programs, like the ones sponsored by the American Culinary Federation Education Foundation (ACFEF), often in partnership with postsecondary institutions. Upon completion of a two-or-three year apprenticeship, students who pass a written and a practical exam qualify for ACF certification as either a certified sous chef (CSC) or a certified culinarian (CC).

An apprenticeship program gives students a paycheck as they receive hands-on training and the book knowledge necessary to be successful in today’s industry.

--Cindy Komarinski, CCC, CCE, HAAC, PhD, Westmoreland Community College, whose ACF-accredited program partners with Nemacolin Woodlands Resort
